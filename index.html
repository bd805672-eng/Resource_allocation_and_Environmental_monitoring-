MINERALS MAPPING

Comprehensive Minerals Mapping Analysis

This minerals mapping study presents a detailed spatial analysis of mineral resource distribution across West Bengal. Using remote sensing data and GIS-based analysis, various metallic and non-metallic minerals such as coal, iron ore, limestone, bauxite, china clay, fire clay, quartz, and feldspar have been identified and classified.

Key Findings:

High Concentration Zones: Significant mineral concentrations are mainly observed in the western districts of West Bengal, particularly Purulia, Bankura, and Paschim Medinipur, along the Chotanagpur Plateau fringe. These areas show strong potential for economically viable mining activities.

Moderate Deposits: Moderate mineral occurrences are found in parts of northern West Bengal, including the Himalayan foothill regions, where minerals such as copper and quartz are present but constrained by terrain and environmental sensitivity.

Low Concentration Areas: The eastern and southern alluvial plains, including Nadia, Murshidabad, and South 24 Parganas, show sparse mineral presence, mainly limited to minor clay and moorum deposits.

AQUIFER DEPTH MAPPING

Aquifer Depth Mapping

The aquifer depth mapping of West Bengal reveals significant spatial variation in groundwater occurrence, controlled by geological formations, geomorphology, and hydrological conditions. The map classifies aquifer depth into five categories ranging from very shallow to very deep.

Very Shallow Aquifers (1–19 m) are predominantly observed in the western districts such as Purulia, Bankura, West Midnapore, and parts of Birbhum. These zones are associated with weathered hard rock terrain and shallow alluvial cover, resulting in high infiltration and easy groundwater accessibility.

Shallow to Moderate Aquifers (19–34 m) occur mainly in Bardhaman, Hooghly, and Murshidabad districts. These areas are influenced by thick alluvial deposits of the Ganga–Bhagirathi river system, which support good groundwater storage and recharge potential.

Moderate Aquifers (34–49 m) are common in Nadia, North 24 Parganas, and Malda districts, where layered alluvial sediments form semi-confined aquifers with moderate recharge conditions.

Deep Aquifers (49–79 m) are concentrated in South 24 Parganas and parts of Howrah and the Kolkata peri-urban region. These zones are characterized by deltaic sediments, clay layers, and intensive groundwater abstraction, leading to increased aquifer depth.

Very Deep Aquifers (79–186 m) are mainly restricted to the coastal South 24 Parganas and Sundarbans region. Thick clay-dominated deltaic deposits, saline water intrusion, and limited freshwater horizons necessitate deep drilling and careful groundwater management.

Overall, the map highlights favorable shallow groundwater conditions in western and central West Bengal, while emphasizing the need for controlled extraction and sustainable management in the southern coastal and deltaic regions.

Water Quality Index (WQI) Map of Purulia District

The Water Quality Index (WQI) map of Purulia district represents the spatial distribution of groundwater quality categorized into five classes: Very Good, Good, Moderate, Bad, and Very Bad. The map clearly indicates considerable spatial variability in groundwater quality across the district, influenced by geological, hydrological, and anthropogenic factors.

Very Good to Good water quality zones (dark and light green) are predominantly observed in the central and southern parts of Purulia. These areas are generally characterized by low population density, forest-covered or rural landscapes, and limited industrial activities, which help maintain better groundwater quality through natural filtration processes.

Moderate water quality zones (yellow) form transitional belts around the good-quality areas. These regions indicate increasing pressure from agricultural activities, fertilizer usage, and localized settlements, leading to moderate levels of groundwater contamination.

Bad to Very Bad water quality zones (orange and red) are mainly concentrated in the southern, south-western, eastern, and north-eastern parts of the district. These areas are associated with hard rock terrain, limited groundwater circulation, agricultural runoff, and improper waste disposal. Elevated concentrations of dissolved solids and chemical parameters contribute to poor water quality in these zones.

Overall, the WQI map suggests that although a large portion of Purulia district has groundwater of acceptable quality, several pockets require immediate management intervention. The results emphasize the need for regular monitoring, appropriate water treatment measures, and sustainable land and water resource management practices to ensure safe drinking water availability.

GROUND WATER POTENTIALITY MAPPING

Groundwater Potentiality Zones of Bankura District

The groundwater potentiality map's spatial variation is mainly governed by geology, slope, drainage density, and proximity to river systems.

Very Good potential zones are distributed along major river courses such as the Damodar, Dwarakeswar, Shilabati, especially in the eastern-southern parts of the district, including Sonamukhi and Indus . These zones consist of alluvial deposits, gentle slopes, high recharge conditions.

Good potential zones occur adjacent to river valleys and low-lying plains in blocks like Kotulpur, Jaypur, and parts of Bishnupur. Moderate permeability and seasonal recharge support sustainable groundwater extraction.

Moderate potential zones dominate central Bankura, including Bankura-I, Bankura-II, and Chhatna blocks. Groundwater occurs mainly within weathered and fractured hard rock formations with limited storage capacity.

Bad to Very Bad potential zones are concentrated in the western and north-western plateau regions such as Ranibandh and Khatra are characterized by steep slopes, crystalline hard rocks, thin soil cover, high surface runoff, resulting in poor groundwater recharge.

Overall, the map indicates that eastern riverine areas are favorable for groundwater development, while western plateau regions require artificial recharge measures and careful groundwater management.
